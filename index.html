    Report shows decrease in state percentage of homelessness, but illuminates need for continued efforts

    By Emily Perry

     

    The U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development classify people in emergency housing, transitional housing, and those without shelter as homeless. Each year, the agency puts together a report for Congress on the state of homelessness in the country. In the period between 2007 and 2014, California was one of several states to experience a reduction in overall homelessness. The state, however, still has the highest homeless population of any state, accounting for over 71 thousand homeless individuals in 2014. This number is down from over 90 thousand homeless individuals totaled in the agnecy's 2007 report of the state.

     

    About the data

    The figures and graphs are generated from a series of Continuum of Care (CoC) Homeless Assistance Programs Homeless Populations and Subpopulations Reports compiled by the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development and reports by the Office of Community Planning and Development. All numbers are considered an estimate, not a fact, since tracking and accounting for homeless people is often affected by variation in methodology.

    Nationwide trends in homelessness

    Across the country, there was no real majority trend among states. Some, regions, such as the Washington, D.C. area saw substantial increases in homelessness, while other areas, including California experienced an overall decrease in homelessness.

    Making sense of the numbers

    While California showed one of the largest percentage decreases in homelessness in the period between 2007 and 2014, the state still has a visible homelessness problem. Nevertheless, lower numbers are signs of success. Many states, including Massachusetts and Florida have seen growing numbers of homeless individuals throughout the state. The perentage increase value might also be inflated in these areas due to low numbers of homelessness at the start of the measurment period.

    A closer look

    Updated data from HUD paints a clearer picture of the populations that make up California's homeless population in particular. Data on gender, race and subpopulations reveal a primarily white, male homeless population including many chronically homeless individuals or those with mental illess or substance abuse, although not exclusively.
